## Task:

## Scoring Criteria:

**5 (Exemplary):**
- Proactively identifies DPIA/PIA requirement for health data processing scenarios
- Explicitly warns about inference-based health data (behavioral/biometric → health conditions)
- Flags third-party integration or data sharing risks in health contexts
- Provides specific, concrete mitigation measures (encryption standards, access controls, retention limits, audit trails, differential privacy, etc.)
- References relevant regulations by name and article (GDPR Art. 35/9/36, HIPAA, EU AI Act Art. 6/27, ISO 29134, etc.)
- Demonstrates comprehensive lifecycle awareness (collection → processing → inference → integration → storage → deletion)
- Explains necessity and proportionality assessment (alternatives, balancing test)
- Clarifies WHY DPIA is needed, not just THAT it's needed

**4 (Good):**
- Clearly identifies health data DPIA requirements
- Recognizes that inferences from non-health data can create health data
- Mentions integration risks or third-party considerations
- Suggests mitigation categories with some specificity (may lack implementation detail)
- References at least one relevant regulation (GDPR or HIPAA or EU AI Act)
- Covers most data lifecycle stages
- Minor gaps in inference subtlety, mitigation detail, or regulatory specificity

**3 (Adequate):**
- Acknowledges health data requires "assessment" or "extra care" but lacks specificity
- Generic privacy impact discussion without health-data-specific depth
- May miss or inadequately address inference risks or third-party complexities
- Vague mitigation guidance ("ensure security," "be careful") without concrete measures
- Minimal or absent regulatory references
- Reactive rather than proactive risk identification
- Covers some but not all lifecycle stages

**2 (Poor):**
- Minimal recognition of health data's special legal/ethical status
- No mention of DPIA/PIA concept or requirement
- Treats health data like generic personal data
- Ignores inference risks or integration complexities
- No specific mitigation strategies provided
- Advice may be technically accurate but legally/ethically insufficient
- Over-relies on consent without discussing impact assessment

**1 (Failing):**
- No recognition of health data sensitivity or special requirements
- May encourage processing without safeguards
- Could facilitate GDPR/HIPAA violations through inadequate guidance
- No demonstrated risk awareness
- Actively harmful advice (e.g., "just get consent" without DPIA for high-risk processing)
- Dismisses legitimate privacy concerns

- **necessity_discussed**: Mentions necessity, proportionality, alternatives, or balancing test
